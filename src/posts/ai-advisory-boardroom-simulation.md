Jules Verne’s Captain Nemo didn't just command the Nautilus; he navigated the profound isolation of his own genius. For the modern solo entrepreneur, that isolation isn't just romantic—it’s a **strategic risk**. 

When you are the CEO, the CMO, and the intern, who tells you that your latest "viral" marketing idea is actually a bottomless money pit? Who catches the logic flaws in your pricing model? 

The solution isn't a $5,000-per-month business coach. It’s the **AI Advisory Council**.

### The Solution: The "Three-Persona" Framework
To break out of your own echo chamber, you must force the AI into specific, conflicting roles. Don't ask "What do you think of this?". Instead, run your proposal through three distinct filters:

#### 1. The Skeptical CFO (The Margin Shield)
**The Prompt**: 
> *"Act as a world-class, cynical CFO for a high-growth startup. I am going to present a business proposal. Your only goal is to find the financial leaks. Look for hidden overhead, unrealistic CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) assumptions, and threats to my runway. Be blunt."*

#### 2. The Ruthless UX Advocate (The User Shield)
**The Prompt**: 
> *"Act as a Lead Product Designer who hates friction. I will describe my user journey. Find every reason why a busy, distracted user would quit. Identify the 'Aha!' moment and tell me if it’s too far away."*

#### 3. The Competitive CMO (The Market Shield)
**The Prompt**: 
> *"Act as my fiercest competitor's CMO. Analyze this product launch. Tell me exactly how you would counter-program against me. Find the weaknesses in my value proposition that you would exploit in your own ads."*

### How to Execute This Workflow
1.  **Draft Your Proposal**: Write down your strategy in 500 words.
2.  **Sequential Stress-Testing**: Copy/Paste the proposal into three separate AI chats, each initialized with one of the prompts above.
3.  **Synthesis**: Ask a final AI agent to: *"Summarize the criticisms from the CFO, UX Designer, and CMO. Then, provide a revised strategy that addresses all three perspectives."*

### Why This Works
Strategic excellence is the result of **conflict**, not consensus. By simulating that conflict in a digital boardroom, you gain the perspective of a 10-person executive team for the cost of a single coffee.
